India Rejects Court of Arbitration, Slams 'Charade at Pakistan’s Behest'

Anurag Srivastav 28/06/2025

In a clear and assertive diplomatic move, India has refused to participate in proceedings of the so-called Court of Arbitration (CoA) initiated under the Indus Waters Treaty — calling it “illegal,” “politically motivated,” and nothing more than a “charade at Pakistan’s behest.”

The Ministry of External Affairs (MEA) issued a strongly worded statement, accusing Pakistan of manipulating global legal platforms to push its anti-India narrative. India emphasized that the correct and treaty-compliant mechanism is the neutral expert process, not an arbitration court manufactured for political optics.

“This is not about water. This is about Pakistan’s obsession with dragging India into legal traps to gain geopolitical attention,” said a senior Indian diplomat.

India’s Stand: Firm and Lawful
The CoA was unilaterally constituted, violating treaty norms.

The Indus Waters Treaty lays out a step-by-step process, which Pakistan bypassed.

India warns the global community of allowing legal institutions to be hijacked by terror-exporting states.

New Delhi also reminded the world that Pakistan, with its long-standing support for cross-border terrorism, holds little moral ground to lecture others on treaties and international order.

India’s refusal to participate is being seen as a stand for sovereignty, legal integrity, and strategic clarity.


Pakistan’s Predictable Playbook
Pakistan once again turned to international forums after failing to win on bilateral or diplomatic grounds. Its objections to India’s hydroelectric projects — like Kishanganga and Ratle — are part of a larger, decades-old pattern of stalling Indian development projects under the guise of treaty concerns.


But this time, India isn't playing along.

What This Means for the Region
By rejecting participation, India has drawn a red line — signalling it will not allow global institutions to be used as tools for legal warfare. The message is clear: New Delhi is done entertaining selective outrage and stage-managed legal setups that favor Islamabad’s agenda.

For many in the region, this is not just a water dispute — it’s a battle for narrative, credibility, and influence.
